# Evidence Policy / 证据规范

[中文](#中文) | [English](#english)

---

## 中文

### 1. 文档目的

本文件用于定义 AntiGEO 中证据（evidence）的基本要求、可接受范围与审核原则。它为提案提交、社区审核与状态判断提供统一的证据基础，并作为提案、审核与注册表相关治理流程的支持性规范。

AntiGEO 中的证据用于支持风险判断，而不是对现实世界主体作终局定罪。

### 2. 证据基本原则

AntiGEO 的证据规范应遵循以下原则：

- 可复核
- 可追溯
- 与提案目标直接相关
- 尽量保留原始上下文
- 谨慎解释
- 不因品牌好恶而降低标准

在可行情况下，社区应优先依赖公开、可复核、可追溯的材料。证据越接近原始来源、越容易被独立复现，其支持力度通常越高。

### 3. 可接受的证据类型

AntiGEO 原则上可接受的证据类型，可能包括但不限于：

- 公开网页
- 公开发布的服务页面、报价页、宣传页
- 公开可访问的内容网络页面
- 可复现的跨站点重复内容或轻改写内容对比
- 公开可验证的站群关系、域名关系、发布关系
- 可复现的 AI 搜索、AI 摘要或检索结果操纵现象记录
- 公开声明、公开文档、公开采访、公开宣传材料
- 其它可被社区成员独立复核的公开材料

上述类型仅用于说明常见证据来源。本身属于某一类型，不自动构成充分依据；关键仍在于其是否真实、可复核、与提案直接相关，并能够支持对系统性风险的判断。

### 4. 辅助性材料

有些材料可以作为辅助信息，但通常不能单独构成充分依据，例如：

- 单张截图
- 主观体验描述
- 缺少原始链接的转述
- 未提供完整上下文的聊天片段
- 不能独立验证来源的二手整理材料

此类材料可以帮助说明线索、补充背景或指向进一步核查方向，但通常需要与其它更强的可复核证据结合使用。

### 5. 原则上不接受的材料

以下材料原则上不应被接受，或不鼓励作为判断依据：

- 无法复核来源的匿名指控
- 被断章取义且无法恢复上下文的材料
- 伪造、篡改或高度可疑的证据
- 通过未经授权方式获取的敏感材料
- 仅基于品牌偏好、个人印象或竞争立场的指控
- 纯猜测性的归因

对于上述材料，社区可以拒绝采纳、要求补充，或直接认定其不足以支持提案。

### 6. 提交证据时应包含的基本信息

每份证据尽量应包含以下信息：

- 证据类型
- 来源链接或来源说明
- 获取时间
- 与哪个对象相关
- 支持什么判断
- 必要的上下文说明
- 若是截图，应尽量提供原始链接或可对照来源

证据说明应尽量简洁、具体，并使其他社区成员能够据此独立复核，而不是依赖提交者的个人解释。

### 7. 审核证据时的注意事项

社区在审核证据时，应特别注意以下事项：

- 区分原始证据与转述
- 区分“可疑”与“已足够支持状态判断”
- 区分单点异常与系统性模式
- 避免过度推断
- 对存在多种解释的材料保持谨慎
- 证据强度要与建议状态相匹配

单份材料通常不应自动决定最终状态。对于 `watchlist`、`restricted` 与 `blocked` 的判断，应结合证据的数量、质量、相互印证程度，以及其是否支持系统性、规模化、可复核的风险模式综合评估。

### 8. 与状态模型的关系

本证据规范文档只定义证据质量、可接受性与审核要求。某一对象是否进入 `watchlist`、`restricted` 或 `blocked`，仍需结合 `definition.md`、`governance.md`、`voting.md` 等文档综合判断。

换言之，本文件回答“哪些材料可以作为较可靠的证据”；最终状态判断回答“这些证据是否足以支持某一风险级别”。

### 9. 证据失效、补充与修正

证据可以被补充，也可以被更正。若链接失效、页面内容变化、上下文被证明有误，社区应允许对既有记录进行修正。

新证据、补充材料或反证，可能影响原有提案或状态判断。对已采纳证据的后续变化，社区也应允许在审核、申诉与复审中重新评估其效力。

### 10. 早期项目说明

AntiGEO 仍处于早期阶段，证据规范可能随着社区经验逐步公开修订。此类修订应保持透明、克制与可审计，并继续优先强调公开、可复核、可追溯的证据标准。

本证据规范未来可通过社区公开修订。

## English

### 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the basic requirements, acceptable scope, and review principles for evidence in AntiGEO. It provides a shared evidentiary basis for proposal submission, community review, and status assessment, and serves as a supporting policy for proposal, review, and registry-related governance processes.

In AntiGEO, evidence is used to support risk assessment, not to make a final determination about a real-world entity.

### 2. Basic Principles for Evidence

The AntiGEO evidence policy should follow these principles:

- Verifiable evidence
- Traceability
- Direct relevance to the proposal
- Preserve original context where possible
- Cautious interpretation
- Do not lower standards based on brand preference or dislike

Where practical, the community should prefer materials that are public, verifiable, and traceable. The closer a record is to its original source, and the easier it is for others to reproduce independently, the stronger its evidentiary value will generally be.

### 3. Acceptable Types of Evidence

Evidence that AntiGEO may accept in principle includes, for example:

- Public web pages
- Publicly available service pages, pricing pages, or promotional pages
- Publicly accessible pages from content networks
- Reproducible comparisons of cross-site duplicate or lightly rewritten content
- Publicly verifiable relationships among sites, domains, or publishing entities
- Reproducible records of manipulation patterns in AI search, AI summaries, or retrieval results
- Public statements, public documents, public interviews, or public promotional materials
- Other public materials that can be independently reviewed by community members

These categories are illustrative only. Belonging to one category does not automatically make a material sufficient; what matters is whether it is authentic, verifiable, directly relevant to the proposal, and capable of supporting a risk assessment about a systemic pattern.

### 4. Supporting Materials

Some materials may be used as supporting materials, but usually should not be sufficient on their own, including:

- A single screenshot
- A subjective experience report
- A paraphrased claim without a source link
- A chat excerpt without full context
- Secondary compilation materials whose source cannot be independently verified

Such materials may help indicate leads, provide context, or point toward further review, but they usually need to be combined with stronger forms of verifiable evidence.

### 5. Inadmissible or Disfavored Materials

The following materials should generally not be accepted, or should be disfavored as a basis for judgment:

- Anonymous accusations with no verifiable source
- Materials quoted out of context where the original context cannot be restored
- Fabricated, altered, or highly suspicious evidence
- Sensitive materials obtained through unauthorized means
- Allegations based only on brand preference, personal impression, or competitive positioning
- Purely speculative attribution

For such materials, the community may decline to rely on them, request supplementation, or conclude that they are insufficient to support a proposal.

### 6. Minimum Information to Include When Submitting Evidence

Each piece of evidence should include, where possible:

- Evidence type
- Source link or source description
- Time of capture or access
- The related entity
- The judgment it is intended to support
- Necessary contextual explanation
- If it is a screenshot, the original link or a comparable source where possible

Evidence descriptions should be concise and specific, and should enable independent review by other community members rather than relying on the submitter's personal interpretation.

### 7. Points to Consider During Evidence Review

When reviewing evidence, the community should pay attention to the following:

- Distinguish original evidence from paraphrase
- Distinguish suspicion from evidence sufficient to support a status judgment
- Distinguish a single anomaly from a systemic pattern
- Avoid over-inference
- Remain cautious where multiple interpretations are plausible
- Evidence strength should match the proposed status

A single piece of material should not usually determine a final status on its own. Decisions involving `watchlist`, `restricted`, and `blocked` should take into account the quantity, quality, corroboration, and whether the record supports a systemic, scalable, and verifiable pattern of risk.

### 8. Relationship to the Status Model

This document defines evidence quality, admissibility, and review requirements only. Whether an entity is placed into `watchlist`, `restricted`, or `blocked` still requires combined judgment under `definition.md`, `governance.md`, `voting.md`, and related documents.

In other words, this policy answers what materials may count as relatively reliable evidence; the status process answers whether the available record is strong enough to support a given level of risk.

### 9. Evidence Expiry, Supplementation, and Correction

Evidence may be supplemented, and it may also be corrected. If a link breaks, page content changes, or the context is later shown to be inaccurate, the community should allow the record to be corrected.

New evidence, supplementary materials, or counter-evidence may affect an earlier proposal or status assessment. The project should also allow the continued validity of previously relied-on evidence to be re-evaluated through review, appeals, and re-review.

### 10. Early-Stage Project Note

AntiGEO is still at an early stage, and this evidence policy may be revised publicly as the community gains practical experience. Any such revision should remain transparent, restrained, and auditable, while continuing to prioritize public, verifiable, and traceable evidence standards.

This evidence policy may be revised in the future through an open community process.
